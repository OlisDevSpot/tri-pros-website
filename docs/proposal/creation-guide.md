# Proposal Creation Guide — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Overview

The proposal is a sales document, not just a quote. It needs to tell a story: here's your problem, here's our solution, here's why we're the right company to do it, here's what it costs, and here's how to move forward.

A well-built proposal does two jobs:
1. **Reinforces** what the customer felt during the in-home meeting
2. **Overcomes** any hesitation that surfaced after you left

Build the proposal within 24 hours of the meeting. Memory fades fast.

---

## The 7 Steps of the Proposal Flow

### Step 1 — Customer Information

**What to include**: Name, address, email(s), phone.

**Key action**: Enter **both** decision-makers' emails if you have them. This ensures both people receive the DocuSign and can review it together.

**Common mistake**: Entering only one email, then the other spouse says "I never saw it" — and the conversation dies.

---

### Step 2 — Scope of Work

**What to include**: The specific trades and line-item scopes discussed in the meeting.

**Rules**:
- Start with the customer's primary concern — the thing they mentioned first or most emphatically
- Add complementary scopes as additional items (not as the headline)
- Do not add scopes you didn't discuss — it creates confusion and looks like you're padding the quote

**Good scope language** (outcome-focused):
> "Complete roof replacement with 30-year architectural shingles, new underlayment, and updated flashing — designed to protect your home for decades and qualify for the manufacturer's full system warranty."

**Bad scope language** (spec-heavy):
> "GAF Timberline HDZ 30yr Architectural Shingle w/ Feltbuster Synthetic Underlayment, 26g flashing, and ridge cap"

Use outcome language in the customer-facing text. Technical specs belong in internal notes or the scope breakdown table.

**Scope pairings to consider** (based on what you learned in the meeting):

| If they asked about... | Consider adding... |
|---|---|
| Roofing | Insulation (attic already accessible) |
| Insulation | HVAC (energy story compounds) |
| Windows | Insulation (complete envelope sealing) |
| Bathroom | Flooring (room-by-room narrative) |
| HVAC | Insulation (efficiency stacks) |

Only add scopes the customer expressed interest in. Don't assume.

---

### Step 3 — Materials Selection

**What to include**: Specific materials selected for each scope, tied to the tier the customer reacted positively to.

**Tip**: If the customer said "quality is most important," lead with mid-tier or premium materials. If they said "budget," lead with standard options but note the upgrade path.

**Common mistake**: Defaulting to the cheapest material automatically — this trains the customer to expect the lowest price and doesn't give you room to negotiate.

---

### Step 4 — Past Results (Portfolio)

**What to include**: 2–3 project examples that are:
- Same trade as the primary scope
- Same home type or area if possible
- Show a before/after if available

**For each project, include**:
- A short description of the problem → solution → outcome
- A customer quote or testimonial if available

**Why this matters**: By the time the customer opens the proposal email at home (possibly two days after the meeting), they may have forgotten the portfolio you showed them in person. Embedding past results in the proposal re-establishes trust at the most important moment — when they're deciding.

---

### Step 5 — Incentives

**What to include**: Any time-bound incentives discussed in the meeting.

**Rules**:
- Only include incentives that are real and currently active
- Specify the expiration date prominently
- Show the value clearly: "$500 off," "Upgrade to premium shingle at no cost," "Free gutter guard installation"

**How to frame it in the proposal**:
> "Available through [Date]: [Incentive description]. This offer is available to customers who complete their agreement by [date]. Please contact your agent to confirm availability."

**Incentive visibility**: The incentive should be visible without scrolling. Don't bury it.

---

### Step 6 — Financing Options

**What to include**: Monthly payment breakdown for 1–2 loan term options.

**Rules**:
- Show the monthly payment prominently — not the total price
- Include multiple term options (e.g., 10-year vs. 15-year) so the customer can choose
- If your loan math supports it, show the daily equivalent: "$X/day"

**See**: `proposal/financing-presentation.md` for framing strategy and `src/shared/lib/loan-calculations.ts` for the monthly payment math.

**Common mistake**: Hiding the financing section or making it hard to find. The monthly payment is often the thing that makes the total price feel manageable — put it front and center.

---

### Step 7 — Agreement / Terms

**What to include**: Scope summary, total contract price, key terms, DocuSign signature block.

**Before sending, verify**:
- [ ] All scope items match what was discussed in the meeting
- [ ] Total price matches the number you quoted verbally
- [ ] Incentive (if applicable) is reflected in the total
- [ ] Both decision-makers' emails are in the DocuSign recipient list
- [ ] Your agent contact info is visible on the document

## Common Proposal Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It Hurts | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Too many scopes | Overwhelms and confuses | Lead with 1–2, make rest optional add-ons |
| No incentive | Removes urgency | Always include a time-bound offer |
| Generic agreement notes | Feels impersonal | Reference specific things from the meeting |
| Only one email address | Spouse says "I never saw it" | Always get both decision-makers' emails |
| Technical scope language | Customer doesn't understand what they're buying | Use outcome statements |
| Proposal built days later | Momentum and details are lost | Build within 24 hours |
| No portfolio examples | Trust evaporates when agent isn't present | Embed 2–3 relevant past projects |

## Using the Proposal During the Meeting (Live Demo Close)

You can build or review the proposal **during the meeting** — particularly useful for the close.

**When to use it**:
- The meeting is going well and you want to demonstrate the tool
- You want to make the scope real and tangible
- You're attempting an on-the-spot close

**How to execute**:
1. After the scope discussion: "Let me pull this up so you can see what the proposal would look like."
2. Walk through the key sections together — scope, past results, financing
3. At the price page: bridge to monthly payment
4. At the agreement: "This is where we'd formalize it. Should we go ahead?"

The live demo close is one of the most effective same-day close techniques because the proposal is already built — there's no "I'll send it later" delay.
