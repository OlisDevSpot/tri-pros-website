# Scope Presentation — Tri Pros Remodeling

## The Problem: Scope Overwhelm

Homeowners are not contractors. When you present six line items covering three trades with material specs and technical qualifications, they feel:

- **Confused** — "I don't know what half of this means"
- **Overwhelmed** — "This is too much to process"
- **Suspicious** — "Why do I need all of this?"

All three states lead to the same outcome: they don't sign.

The solution is deliberate scope simplification — not because you're hiding anything, but because a customer who understands what they're buying is dramatically more likely to buy it.

---

## The 1-2-3 Scope Rule

**Start with their #1 pain point.** This is the thing they mentioned first, mentioned most emotionally, or that is most urgent (visible damage, active leak, spike in energy bills).

**Add one complementary scope** that either:
- Naturally pairs with the first (attic insulation + roofing, HVAC + insulation)
- Addresses a secondary concern they mentioned in discovery
- Significantly increases value at relatively low added cost

**Make everything else optional add-ons.** Do not lead with them. After the first two are agreed, you can mention: "There are a couple of additional things I want to show you — but these are separate and completely optional. Let's make sure we're aligned on the core first."

This keeps the customer focused, reduces cognitive load, and prevents scope confusion from killing the close.

**Framework connection**: The 1-2-3 Scope Rule is TPR's version of Grand Slam Offer Stacking — the primary scope is the core offer, the complementary scope is the bonus, and everything else is optional. See `sales/sales-frameworks.md`.

## Outcome Statements vs. Technical Specs

Every scope item should be explained in terms of what it means for the homeowner's life — not what it is as a product or installation.

### Formula

> "[Technical thing we're doing] means [outcome for them] — which addresses [problem they mentioned]."

### Examples

| Scope | Technical description | Outcome statement |
|---|---|---|
| Roofing | GAF Timberline 30-year architectural shingle replacement | "Your home will be fully protected from weather damage for the next 30 years — no more worrying about the next storm." |
| Insulation | R-38 blown-in fiberglass + air sealing in attic | "You'll notice the comfort difference almost immediately — and most customers see their heating and cooling bills drop by 20–40%." |
| Windows | Dual-pane Low-E vinyl window replacement | "No more drafts, no more noise from outside, and your home stays at a consistent temperature without your HVAC working overtime." |
| HVAC | 18-SEER heat pump replacement + smart thermostat | "Your system will use roughly half the energy of your current unit — and you'll be able to control it from your phone." |
| Solar | 6kW rooftop solar + microinverters + monitoring | "You're locking in your energy rate for 25 years while your neighbors keep paying whatever the utility charges." |
| Bathroom remodel | Full tear-out, subway tile shower, new vanity, LVP | "You'll have a completely updated bathroom — like moving into a new house, without moving." |
| Foundation repair | Helical pier installation + crack sealing | "Your home will be structurally stable, and when you go to sell it, it will pass inspection without any issues." |

## Trade-Specific Scope Pairings

These scope combinations tell a single, coherent story to the customer:

| Primary scope | Add this | The story |
|---|---|---|
| Roofing | Insulation | "While the attic is open, we can upgrade insulation — one crew visit, compounding energy savings." |
| Roofing | Solar | "A new roof is the ideal foundation for solar — one installation sequence, better ROI." |
| Insulation | HVAC | "Sealing the envelope and upgrading the system — your energy bills drop dramatically both ways." |
| Windows | Insulation | "Complete envelope sealing — the most cost-effective energy upgrade you can do." |
| HVAC | Windows | "Your new system won't have to fight through drafty windows — both upgrades work together." |
| Bathroom | Flooring | "As long as we're updating the bathroom, the transition to new flooring in the hallway / bedroom is natural and seamless." |

**Rule**: Only propose a pairing if the customer expressed interest in both or if the pairing is genuinely in their interest. Do not bundle to inflate the contract at the customer's expense.

## Grand Slam Offer Stacking

Instead of presenting scope as a line-item list, name and stack each component to increase perceived value:

| Instead of | Stack it as |
|---|---|
| "Roof replacement — $18,000" | "The Complete Protection Package: 30-year architectural shingle system + new underlayment + updated flashing + ridge vent upgrade + full permit and inspection + 10-year workmanship warranty + manufacturer warranty pass-through" |
| "Insulation — $8,500" | "The Home Comfort Upgrade: R-38 blown-in insulation + full air sealing + attic access restoration + thermal barrier + immediate comfort improvement + 20-40% energy bill reduction" |

Each named component increases perceived value without changing the deliverable. This is the same technique used in `sales/sales-frameworks.md` — Grand Slam Offer principles.

### Scarcity Signals

When presenting scope, you can add legitimate scarcity signals:
- "Our roofing crew has one opening in [month] — if we lock in your project this week, we can get you on the calendar."
- "We only take on [X] projects per month in your area to maintain quality."
- "Material pricing is locked for [timeframe] — after that, costs go up with the supplier."

Only use real scarcity. Fake scarcity destroys trust permanently.

## Presenting Scope During the Meeting

### Step 1 — Anchor to what they told you

Never start the scope presentation without referencing what the customer said in discovery.

> "Based on what you told me — [their primary concern] — here's what I'd recommend..."

This signals that you listened. Customers who feel heard are more receptive.

### Step 2 — Present the primary scope first

One scope. Explain it in outcome language. Pause and let them react.

> "Does that address the main thing you were concerned about?"

Wait for confirmation before moving on.

### Step 3 — Introduce the complementary scope as a bonus

> "There's one more thing I want to show you — and it's optional. But given what we're already doing with [primary scope], it makes a lot of sense to consider [secondary scope] at the same time. Here's why..."

Framing it as optional lowers defensiveness. Most customers accept the pairing when it's presented this way.

### Step 4 — Pause before pricing

Do not transition directly from scope to price. Ask:

> "Does this feel like the right direction for what your home needs?"

You want a verbal "yes" — or at minimum, no resistance — before introducing any numbers. A customer who hasn't committed to the solution will use the price as an excuse to exit.

## Addressing Scope Confusion in the Proposal

If a customer reopens a proposal and says they're confused about what's included:

1. **Offer a walkthrough**: "Can I spend 15 minutes on a screen share to walk through it with you?" — this almost always resolves confusion and re-warms the prospect
2. **Simplify verbally**: "Ignore everything except the first two line items — that's the core of what we discussed. The rest is optional."
3. **Offer to rebuild**: If the proposal is genuinely too complex, rebuild a simplified version focused on 1–2 scopes

A confused customer is still a customer. The goal is to get them back on solid ground — not to defend the complexity of the original proposal.

## The Energy-Efficient Framing

For energy-efficient scopes, there is a powerful overlay that addresses both scope complexity and price objection simultaneously:

> "Here's how to think about this: your home currently has [problem — e.g., an old HVAC system + minimal insulation]. Every month, you're paying [current energy bill amount] — and that money is gone. What we're doing is converting that monthly waste into an investment that generates returns for 20–25 years.
>
> The scope isn't 'things to do to your house.' It's the package of improvements that eliminates the inefficiency. Every item has a measurable monthly savings attached to it."

This reframes the scope as a financial decision rather than a construction decision — which often resolves both confusion and price sensitivity in one move.

**Value Equation connection**: This framing hits all four variables:
- **Dream Outcome**: "An investment generating monthly returns"
- **Perceived Likelihood**: "Measurable monthly savings attached to it" (quantifiable)
- **Time Delay**: "20-25 year investment" (long-term framing reduces urgency anxiety)
- **Effort & Sacrifice**: "Eliminates the inefficiency" (passive benefit — no ongoing work)
