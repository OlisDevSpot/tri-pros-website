# Energy-Saver Incentive Program — Pitch Script & Deal Structure

**Program ID**: `energy-saver`
**Best For**: Homeowners with high utility bills or comfort issues
**Accent Color**: Sky blue

---

## Who This Program Is For

Use the Energy-Saver program when:
- The customer mentioned high utility bills during Phase 1 ($200+/month average)
- They have comfort complaints: rooms that are too hot, too cold, or drafty
- They expressed interest in rebates, tax credits, or "green" upgrades
- Their home has visible signs of energy inefficiency (older windows, minimal insulation, aging HVAC)
- They asked about "paying for itself" or ROI — they're financial-minded

This program frames the project as a financial decision, not an aesthetic one.

---

## Qualifying Signals from Phase 1

- "Our AC runs all day in the summer"
- "It's always freezing upstairs / the back bedroom"
- "We've heard about rebates for insulation/windows"
- "Our SCE bill gets ridiculous in July and August"
- "What would happen if we did the attic?"
- Visible single-pane windows or gaps around doors

---

## Deal Structure

The Energy-Saver Incentive Program bundles:

1. **Energy envelope upgrade** — up to three components: attic insulation, window sealing or replacement, HVAC service or upgrade
2. **Rebate and credit application assistance** — we help the homeowner identify and apply for all applicable federal IRA credits and local utility rebates (LADWP, SoCalGas, SCE)
3. **HERS-certified energy audit** — included at no charge; documents baseline and projected savings
4. **Workmanship warranty** — standard coverage on all installed work

**Typical project cost (after incentives)**: $8,000–$22,000 depending on scope
**Typical incentives applied**: $600–$3,200 (federal IRA tax credit + utility rebates)
**Financing**: 60-month term. Most projects at $150–$350/month after incentives.
**Payback period**: 6–18 months in most cases

**Important**: Federal IRA credits apply on a tax-year basis. Utility rebates (LADWP, SoCalGas, SCE) are first-come, first-served and have annual caps. Timing matters.

## Step-by-Step Pitch Script

### Step 1: The Problem

> "Based on what you've told me, your home is spending somewhere between $100 and $250 every month that it doesn't have to.
>
> That's not just a comfort issue — it's a financial leak. And it's been running since the day you moved in.
>
> The good news: we know exactly where it's coming from, and a significant portion of the fix is covered by federal and state programs right now."

**What to do**: Tie the problem to their specific situation. If they mentioned $320/month AC bills, use that number. Specificity is credibility.

---

### Step 2: The Solution

> "What we do is address the full energy envelope — not just one piece.
>
> Most companies come in and say 'you need new windows' or 'you need insulation.' But those things work together. Better insulation makes your HVAC more efficient. Better windows reduce the radiant heat that your HVAC has to fight.
>
> When we do all three — insulation, windows, and HVAC — the effect compounds. Families typically see 30 to 55 percent reductions in their heating and cooling costs.
>
> One contractor. One mobilization. One invoice."

**What to do**: Emphasize the compounding effect. This is the key differentiator — most competitors only address one thing. Draw this out on paper if the customer is analytical.

---

### Step 3: What the Government Covers

> "Right now, the federal Inflation Reduction Act provides up to $3,200 in annual tax credits for exactly these types of upgrades.
>
> On top of that, LADWP and SoCalGas run their own rebate programs. Combined, qualifying families can offset 25 to 45 percent of the total project cost.
>
> These programs have caps, though. When the annual cap fills up, the rebate is gone. The federal credit runs through 2032, but utility rebates reset and cap annually — and they move fast."

**What to do**: Show the program one by one. Don't lump them together. Let each rebate land as its own piece of good news.

---

### Step 4: Your Monthly Math

> "Let's look at the actual before-and-after.
>
> Right now, based on what you've described, you're spending roughly [estimate from Phase 1] per month in cooling and heating.
>
> After the upgrade, families in similar homes typically see their costs drop to around [projected amount]. That's a reduction of [X] per month — [Y] per year.
>
> The monthly payment on the financing, at 60 months, is [calculated]. That means within about [payback months] months, the utility savings are covering the full payment. After that, you're ahead every single month."

**What to do**: Do the math out loud, using their actual Phase 1 data. Write the numbers down on paper and leave it in front of them.

---

### Step 5: Why Now

> "Here's the piece most people miss.
>
> The IRA credits are reliable — they run through 2032. But the utility rebate programs adjust every year. LADWP hit its 2024 cap in October. Families who called in November — who qualified — lost $600 they were entitled to.
>
> The savings math already works without the rebates. But if we can apply them — and we should try — the right time to apply is now, while the programs are open.
>
> This month, the programs are active. Let's lock it in."

**What to do**: Let the loss-framing sit. The LADWP cap story is real and compelling. The customer should understand that waiting has a documented downside.

## Objection Handling

### "I want to see my utility bills first."

> "That makes total sense. Let's do this — I'll put together the full proposal based on what we've estimated today, and I'll make it conditional on the utility review. Once you've pulled the bills, we can confirm the baseline and finalize the numbers.
>
> That way you're not committed to anything, but we have a plan ready to go."

---

### "How do I know the savings are real?"

> "Fair question. We include a HERS-certified energy audit with every energy project — that's a certified third-party assessment of your home's current efficiency and projected improvement. The savings estimate comes from that audit, not from us guessing.
>
> You'd have a documented baseline and a documented projection. If the numbers don't check out, we'd know before we started."

---

### "I've heard the rebates are complicated to get."

> "They can be, if you do it yourself. We handle the paperwork. Our team files the utility rebate applications directly — you just sign the forms. The federal IRA credit gets documented on your tax return by your preparer — we give you the paperwork they need.
>
> Most customers we work with see the rebates credited within 60–90 days."

---

### "What if my bills don't go down as much as projected?"

> "The projection comes from a HERS audit, which is a recognized standard in the industry. If the efficiency measures we install are performing correctly and your bills don't match the projection, we investigate why — and we stand behind our work.
>
> That's what the workmanship warranty is for. The performance guarantee covers the installation. The math is based on certified methodology."

## Case Study: The Thompson Family — La Puente, CA

**Situation**: Three-bedroom home, built in 1972. Original single-pane windows, almost no attic insulation. Average utility bill: $340/month.

**Program**: Energy-Saver Incentive

**Scope**: Attic insulation (R-38), window replacement (12 double-pane units), HVAC service + smart thermostat

**Incentives applied**:
- IRA tax credit: $1,600
- SoCalGas rebate: $400
- Total offset: $2,000 (32% of project cost)

**Results**:
- Utility bill: $340/month → $190/month average (44% reduction)
- Monthly payment: $215 at 60 months
- Break-even: month 8 post-install

**Quote**: *"Month 8 post-install, the savings covered the full monthly payment. Month 9, we were making money."*
