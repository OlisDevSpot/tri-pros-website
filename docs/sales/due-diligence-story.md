# The Due Diligence Story — Customer Journey Narrative

## Overview

This is the core narrative framework for the in-home meeting flow. It reframes the meeting from a sales pitch into an education — teaching the homeowner what a legitimate, successful construction project actually requires. The agent isn't selling; the agent is showing the homeowner how to protect themselves, and TPR happens to be the company that checks every box.

**When to reference this**: Any work on the meeting flow, meeting steps, program presentations, or customer-facing content that guides the homeowner through making a decision.

---

## The Story

A successful construction project doesn't start on demolition day — it starts when you do your due diligence:

### 1. Proper Licensing & Permits
Your contractor should own and hold the proper license for your project, and pull the right permit with the right trade. Licensed and insured is the bare minimum.

### 2. Clear Scope of Work
Demand a clear and clearly-defined scope of work. This protects you and eliminates many discrepancies that can happen along the way.

### 3. Proper Supervision
Make sure you have proper supervision on your project. We recommend at least 2 sets of eyes — they will ensure they measure twice and cut once.

### 4. Communication
Make sure you know how communication will work on this project. Know who your point of contact is and what to expect.

### 5. Office Support
Make sure you have enough office support. Know what your contractor is offering you and at what quality.

### 6. Proof of Performance
Make sure you check for solid proof of performance. Ask to see reference projects, testimonials, past clients, and relevant customer successes.

## The Closing Truth

> A successful remodeling project is not always about the finishes and nice design — many times success boils down to communication, supervision, leadership, and accountability.
>
> All of this will ensure you have the real deal.

## Psychological Mechanics

This story is effective because it layers multiple persuasion principles into what feels like genuine consumer education. Below is a breakdown of each psychological lever at work.

### 1. Authority Transfer — "I'm the expert, let me teach you"

The agent positions themselves as a knowledgeable advisor, not a salesperson. By teaching the homeowner *how to evaluate any contractor*, the agent earns authority. The homeowner's internal response shifts from "this person wants my money" to "this person knows what they're talking about."

**Lever**: Authority bias (Cialdini). People defer to perceived experts.

**In the story**: Every step is framed as advice the homeowner should apply to *any* contractor — which paradoxically makes them trust *this* contractor more.

### 2. Fear Inoculation — "Here's what goes wrong when you skip this"

Each step implicitly paints a picture of what happens when a contractor *doesn't* do these things: unlicensed work gets torn out, vague scopes lead to disputes, unsupervised crews make mistakes, no communication means anxiety, no proof of performance means you're gambling.

**Lever**: Loss aversion + risk aversion. The homeowner isn't just learning what to look for — they're learning what to be afraid of.

**In the story**: "Eliminates many discrepancies" (what discrepancies? the homeowner's imagination fills in the worst case). "Measure twice and cut once" (implying other contractors don't).

### 3. Contrast Effect — "We check every box; they don't"

The story establishes a 6-point checklist. Once the homeowner internalizes these criteria, every competing contractor is now measured against them. Most cheap contractors fail on 4 out of 6. The homeowner doesn't need TPR to say "we're better" — the checklist does it for them.

**Lever**: Anchoring + contrast principle. The evaluation framework itself is the sales tool.

**In the story**: The framework is presented as objective consumer protection. But TPR designed the criteria around their own strengths.

### 4. Commitment & Consistency — "You agreed these matter"

As the agent walks through each step, the homeowner is nodding along — "yes, licensing matters," "yes, I want clear scope," "yes, supervision is important." Each nod is a micro-commitment. By step 6, the homeowner has verbally (or mentally) agreed to a set of standards that TPR meets and budget contractors don't.

**Lever**: Commitment/consistency (Cialdini). Once people commit to a standard, they feel compelled to act consistently with it.

**In the story**: The agent can later reference these commitments: "You mentioned supervision was important to you — here's exactly how we handle that."

### 5. Identity Alignment — "Smart homeowners do this"

The story frames due diligence as what a responsible, intelligent homeowner does. Choosing a cheap, unlicensed contractor is implicitly framed as naive. The homeowner doesn't want to be that person.

**Lever**: Identity-based persuasion. People make decisions consistent with who they believe they are.

**In the story**: "A successful construction project doesn't start on demolition day" — this implies that homeowners who skip due diligence are setting themselves up for failure. Nobody wants to be that homeowner.

### 6. The Reframe — "Success isn't about finishes"

The closing truth redefines what "success" means. Most homeowners walk in thinking about tile colors and countertop materials. The story redirects them to think about communication, supervision, leadership, and accountability — which are TPR's actual differentiators and harder for competitors to fake.

**Lever**: Framing effect. Whoever defines the evaluation criteria wins the evaluation.

**In the story**: By shifting the definition of success away from aesthetics (where every contractor claims to be good) to operations (where TPR has a structured advantage), the homeowner's decision framework favors TPR before price is ever discussed.

## Optimal Positioning: After the Circle of Pain

The due diligence story is most powerful when it follows the Circle of Pain — the CLOSER O step where the customer relives every failed past experience with contractors.

**Why**: Credentials presented cold ("Let me tell you about our company") feel like a sales pitch. Credentials presented after pain exploration ("Here's why we're different from what you just described") feel like the answer.

**The transition**:
> "That's actually why we built our whole process around preventing exactly what you described. Can I walk you through what a legitimate project actually requires?"

The customer has just spent 5-10 minutes reliving unlicensed work, vague scopes, no supervision, and no communication. Now each due diligence point hits with emotional weight:

| Due Diligence Step | What the customer just relived | Why it lands differently |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Licensing & Permits | "The last guy wasn't even licensed" | Proof this won't happen again |
| 2. Clear Scope of Work | "We never got anything in writing" | Protection from what burned them |
| 3. Proper Supervision | "Nobody was watching the crew" | Direct contrast to their experience |
| 4. Communication | "I couldn't reach anyone for weeks" | The opposite of what they suffered |
| 5. Office Support | "There was no office — just a guy with a truck" | Legitimacy they've been craving |
| 6. Proof of Performance | "I never saw any past work" | Evidence that eliminates the leap of faith |
