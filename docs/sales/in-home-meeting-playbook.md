# In-Home Meeting Playbook — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Overview

The in-home meeting is the primary sales event in TPR's funnel. Everything before it (lead gen, appointment setting) exists to get us in the room. Everything after it (proposal, follow-up, close) is cleanup.

**Your goal**: Understand the customer's needs deeply enough to present a compelling solution — then ask for the business.

**Ideal outcome**: Signed contract before you leave.

**Acceptable outcome**: Proposal built and sent within 24 hours, with a specific follow-up time agreed.

**Framework**: This playbook follows the CLOSER framework — Clarify, Label, Overview (Circle of Pain), Sell the Vacation, Explain Concerns, Reinforce. See `sales/sales-frameworks.md` for the full framework reference.

---

## Pre-Meeting Checklist (Before You Arrive)

### Logistics
- [ ] Confirm both decision-makers will be present (call/text day before if needed)
- [ ] Review CRM notes from lead entry: source, stated concerns, timeline mentioned
- [ ] Know the address — look it up on Google Maps for any visible exterior issues
- [ ] Charged laptop or tablet with proposal system accessible
- [ ] Material samples for the relevant trade(s)
- [ ] Portfolio loaded (2-3 projects from the same trade the customer needs)
- [ ] DocuSign accessible and ready (for same-day close)

### Conviction Check
Before you walk in the door, ask yourself:
- [ ] Do I genuinely believe TPR is the best choice for this customer?
- [ ] If they don't hire us, what happens to them? Who do they end up with?
- [ ] Is closing this deal in the customer's best interest — not just mine?

If you can't answer yes to all three, you're not ready. Conviction transfers through tonality — if you don't believe it, they won't either. See `sales/sales-frameworks.md` — Conviction Framework.

> **Critical**: If only one decision-maker will be present, acknowledge this upfront and plan for a follow-up with both. Never build a full proposal for someone who can't sign.

## Phase 1 — Clarify & Discover (10-15 min)

**CLOSER step**: C (Clarify) + discovery intake

### The Clarify Opening

Before you walk the home, before you ask discovery questions — get the customer to own their problem.

**Opening line**:
> "Before we get into anything — what made you decide to set up this meeting? What are we trying to solve?"

Let them answer fully. Don't interrupt. Their answer tells you:
- What they think the problem is (which may not be the actual problem)
- How urgent it feels to them
- Whether they've already decided to act or are "just looking"

**If they say "I'm just getting information"**: Dig deeper.
> "Totally fair. What specifically are you hoping to learn? What would help you feel confident about making a decision?"

**Goal**: The customer has articulated, in their own words, why they need help. They've owned the problem. You cannot solve someone's problem until they admit they have one.

### The "Walk the Home" Technique

After the clarify opening, ask to see the area of concern before sitting down. Walking with them shifts the dynamic — you're a professional assessing their home, not a salesperson pitching at a kitchen table.

> "Great — would you mind showing me the area you're most concerned about? I want to see it firsthand before we go over any options."

### Discovery Questions

Use open questions. Let them talk. Take brief notes.

1. **Pain point**: "What made you decide to look into this now?"
2. **Timeline**: "Is there a particular time frame you're working with?"
3. **Budget signal**: "Have you given any thought to what kind of budget you're working with for this?"
4. **Priority**: "If you had to rank them — price, quality, or getting it done quickly — which matters most to you?"
5. **Past experience**: "Have you worked with contractors before? How did that go?" *(This transitions naturally into Phase 2)*
6. **Decision process**: "Besides yourself, who else will be involved in making this decision?"

> **Listen for**: urgency signals (roof is leaking, selling the house soon), hesitations (spouse not present, waiting for tax return), and priority alignment (quality-first vs. budget-first).

### What to Confirm Before Moving On

- You understand the primary problem — in their words, not yours
- You know who the decision-makers are and whether both are present
- You have a sense of whether they're price-sensitive or value-oriented
- You know their timeline
- You've opened the door to past contractor experiences

For the full customer intelligence capture, see `sales/customer-intelligence.md`.

## Phase 2 — Label & Circle of Pain (10-15 min)

**CLOSER steps**: L (Label) + O (Overview / Circle of Pain)

### Label Their Situation

Based on everything you heard in Phase 1, reflect it back as a diagnosis. Make the abstract concrete.

**Script**:
> "Here's what I'm seeing: you've got a [specific problem — e.g., 25-year-old roof / home that was built before modern insulation standards / HVAC system that's working twice as hard as it should]. That's [consequence — costing you $X/month in wasted energy / putting your home at risk every storm season / making your family uncomfortable year-round]. You're at the point where doing nothing costs more than fixing it."

Then confirm:
> "Does that sound right? Am I reading the situation correctly?"

**Why this matters**: When customers hear their problem described accurately, they feel understood. Feeling understood is the fastest trust builder — faster than any credential.

### The Circle of Pain

If they mentioned past contractor experience in Phase 1, go deeper. If they didn't, ask now.

> "You mentioned you've worked with contractors before. Tell me more about that — what happened?"

**Go deep on every failure**:
> "What went wrong?"
> "What did that end up costing you — in money, time, stress?"
> "Was there anything else you tried?"

**Keep asking until they've exhausted their history**:
> "Anything else? Any other experiences — even from neighbors or friends?"

**Summarize the pattern**:
> "So you've tried [X] and [Y], and both times you ended up with [Z]. That's exactly what I hear from a lot of homeowners."

### The Transition

The Circle of Pain creates the perfect setup for the due diligence story:

> "That's actually why we built our whole process around preventing exactly what you described. Can I take a few minutes to walk you through what a legitimate project actually requires?"

The customer is now emotionally primed to hear about licensing, scope clarity, supervision, communication, office support, and proof of performance — because they just relived what happens without them.

For the full Circle of Pain technique, see `sales/sales-frameworks.md`. For the due diligence narrative, see `sales/due-diligence-story.md`.

## Phase 3 — Due Diligence / Trusted Contractor (5-10 min)

**CLOSER context**: This phase builds Perceived Likelihood (Value Equation) — proving that the outcome will actually happen.

### Why This Phase Comes After the Circle of Pain

Credentials presented cold ("Let me tell you about our company") feel like a sales pitch. Credentials presented after pain exploration ("Here's why we're different from what you just described") feel like the answer.

### The Due Diligence Story

Walk through the 6-point framework. For each point, connect it to what they told you in Phase 2.

1. **Licensing & Permits**: "You mentioned the last contractor wasn't licensed. We hold CSLB license [X] — here it is. We pull every permit so the city verifies the work."
2. **Clear Scope of Work**: "You said the last project had scope confusion. Our proposals spell out exactly what's included — outcome by outcome."
3. **Proper Supervision**: "You experienced quality issues. We put two sets of eyes on every project — our foreman and a project manager."
4. **Communication**: "You felt left in the dark. With us, you have one point of contact — their name, number, and email — and we update you proactively."
5. **Office Support**: "We have a full office team handling scheduling, materials, permits, and logistics so nothing falls through the cracks."
6. **Proof of Performance**: Show 2-3 relevant portfolio projects. For each: "This customer had the same issue — here's what we did and what they said."

> **Keep this under 10 minutes.** This is your credentials, not your pitch. The customer already wants a solution — you're proving you can deliver.

For the full narrative and psychological mechanics, see `sales/due-diligence-story.md`.

## Phase 4 — Sell the Vacation / Scope Presentation (15-20 min)

**CLOSER step**: S (Sell the Vacation)

### The Rule

Sell the destination, not the ticket. Customers don't care about R-38 insulation values or SEER ratings. They care about comfort, savings, and peace of mind.

### Before Presenting Scope — Recap C-L-O

> "So let me make sure we're on the same page. You came to us because [Clarify — their stated problem]. I can see that you're dealing with [Label — your diagnosis]. You've tried [Overview — past experiences] and it didn't work out. Now you want [desired outcome]. Let me show you how we'd get you there."

### Rules for This Phase

1. **Start with 1-2 scopes only** — their #1 pain point plus one natural complement
2. **Use outcome language, not technical language**: "This will keep your home 15 degrees cooler in summer" not "R-38 blown-in insulation with air sealing"
3. **Tie every scope back to what they told you in discovery**: "You mentioned energy bills were a big concern — this is specifically what addresses that."

### Example Outcome Statements by Trade

| Trade | Outcome statement |
|---|---|
| Roofing | "Your home will be fully protected from weather damage for the next 30 years, and the cool-roof system will reduce attic heat by up to 30%." |
| Insulation | "You'll notice the difference in comfort within the first month, and most customers see their energy bill drop by 20-40%." |
| Windows | "No more drafts, no more noise from outside, and your home will hold temperature much more consistently." |
| HVAC | "Your system will be efficient enough that most customers cut their cooling bill nearly in half." |
| Solar | "You're essentially locking in your energy rate for 25 years — while your neighbors keep seeing their bills go up." |
| Bathroom | "You'll have a completely updated bathroom that feels brand new — and it adds significantly to your home's resale value." |

### Natural Scope Pairings (for upsells)

- Insulation + HVAC (energy savings compound)
- Roofing redeck + Insulation (attic is already accessible)
- Windows + Insulation (envelope sealing — complete comfort story)
- Bathroom + Full home Flooring (natural extension room-by-room)

For scope pairing details and presentation technique, see `proposal/scope-presentation.md`.

## Phase 5 — Pricing & Financing (10-15 min)

**Value Equation focus**: Decrease the denominator — reduce perceived effort and time delay.

### Rules for This Phase

1. **Never lead with total price** — always lead with value
2. **Present total price only after scope is agreed** — the customer should want it before you quote it
3. **Immediately bridge to monthly payment** — the moment you say the total
4. **Introduce incentives as a same-day decision driver**
5. **Emphasize speed and ease** — "We can get started as early as [date]" and "You don't have to manage anything — we handle it all"

### The Price Bridge Script

> "The total investment for everything we discussed is $[X]. Now, most of our customers don't pay that all at once — we work with financing options that get you into this for around $[monthly payment]/month. That's less than a car payment to have [outcome they care about most]."

Then stop talking. Let that land.

### Effort Reduction (Value Equation — Denominator)

After the price bridge, reinforce how easy this is for them:

> "And just so you know — once you give the go-ahead, we handle everything. Permits, materials, scheduling, crews, cleanup. You don't have to manage any of it. We'll introduce you to your project manager and keep you updated every step of the way."

### Timeline (Value Equation — Denominator)

> "If we get started this week, we can typically have [scope] completed within [timeframe]. You'd start seeing the results almost immediately."

### Incentive Structure

TPR incentives should be:
- **Real** — don't fabricate urgency
- **Time-bound** — expiring at a specific date or tied to a specific condition
- **Meaningful** — a dollar amount, an upgrade, or a free add-on

> "I do want to mention — we have a promotion running through [end of month]. If we can get the paperwork started today, we can include [incentive] at no additional cost. That's something I can't guarantee next week."

For monthly payment math, see `proposal/financing-presentation.md` and `src/shared/lib/loan-calculations.ts`.

## Phase 6 — Close & Explain Concerns (10 min)

**CLOSER steps**: E (Explain Away Concerns) + Close

### The Direct Ask

Do not ask "What do you think?" Ask for the business.

> "Based on everything we've talked about — your situation, what we'd do to fix it, and the investment — I think this is a really strong fit for your home. What would you need to feel comfortable moving forward today?"

Or, for the assumptive close:

> "Let's go ahead and get the paperwork started. I can pull up the agreement right now — it only takes a few minutes to go through."

For close technique details, see `sales/closing-strategies.md`.

### When Objections Surface

This is expected. 90% of customers don't say yes immediately.

**Step 1 — Diagnose the category**:

| Category | Sounds like | Real concern |
|---|---|---|
| External blame | "Can't afford it," "bad timing" | They don't see the value yet |
| Other-person dependency | "Need to talk to spouse" | They don't want to own the decision |
| Internal conflict | "Not sure it's right" | Self-doubt, fear of commitment |

**Step 2 — Apply A.R.A.C.**:
1. **Acknowledge**: Validate without agreeing
2. **Reframe**: Logical reframe (price → monthly, cost → investment)
3. **Associate**: Story of a similar customer (see `sales/story-bank.md`)
4. **Close**: Move toward next step

For scripted responses to every common objection, see `sales/objection-handlers.md`.

### If They Are Not Closing Today

Get a specific next step before you leave — not "I'll think about it."

> "I completely understand. When do you think you'll have a chance to sit down together and review this? Let's set a specific time for a quick follow-up call — 15 minutes. I'll send the proposal in the meantime so you have everything in writing."

Do not leave without:
- A proposal email address confirmed (both decision-makers)
- A specific follow-up date/time agreed
- Notes on what they said they needed to think about

## Phase 7 — Reinforce (Immediately After)

**CLOSER step**: R (Reinforce)

### If They Signed

Begin the post-signing reinforcement sequence immediately:

1. **Before you leave**: Thank them personally. "You made a great decision. Here's what happens next."
2. **Within the hour**: Send a thank-you text
3. **Within 2 hours**: Send the welcome email with "What Happens Next"

The full post-signing protocol is in `sales/post-signing-sequence.md`.

### If They Didn't Sign

Begin the follow-up cadence:

1. **Before you leave**: Confirm next step, get both email addresses, set follow-up time
2. **Same day**: Build and send the proposal
3. **Day 1**: Watch for the proposal view notification — call within 1-2 hours of the trigger

The full follow-up protocol is in `sales/follow-up-cadence.md`.

## Post-Meeting Actions (Within 24 Hours)

- [ ] Create proposal in system — use everything you learned in the meeting
- [ ] Send proposal via DocuSign
- [ ] Log deal in CRM (move to appropriate stage)
- [ ] Create follow-up task for the agreed date/time
- [ ] Note objections, concerns, and what the customer emphasized — use these in follow-up
- [ ] If signed: trigger post-signing sequence

## Phase Flow Summary

| Phase | CLOSER | Time | Goal |
|---|---|---|---|
| Pre-Meeting | Conviction | Before | Believe you're here to help, not sell |
| Phase 1 | C (Clarify) + Discovery | 10-15 min | Customer owns their problem |
| Phase 2 | L (Label) + O (Circle of Pain) | 10-15 min | Problem named, past failures explored |
| Phase 3 | Trust (Due Diligence) | 5-10 min | TPR proven as different from what failed |
| Phase 4 | S (Sell the Vacation) | 15-20 min | Outcome presented, scope agreed |
| Phase 5 | Pricing | 10-15 min | Value > price, monthly payment, effort/speed |
| Phase 6 | E (Explain) + Close | 10 min | Objections handled, decision requested |
| Phase 7 | R (Reinforce) | Ongoing | Buyer's remorse prevented, next steps clear |
