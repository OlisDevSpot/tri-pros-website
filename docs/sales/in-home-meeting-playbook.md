# In-Home Meeting Playbook — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Overview

The in-home meeting is the primary sales event in TPR's funnel. Everything before it (lead gen, appointment setting) exists to get us in the room. Everything after it (proposal, follow-up, close) is cleanup.

**Your goal**: Understand the customer's needs deeply enough to present a compelling solution — then ask for the business.

**Ideal outcome**: Signed contract before you leave.

**Acceptable outcome**: Proposal built and sent within 24 hours, with a specific follow-up time agreed.

---

## Pre-Meeting Checklist (Before You Arrive)

- [ ] Confirm both decision-makers will be present (call/text day before if needed)
- [ ] Review CRM notes from lead entry: source, stated concerns, timeline mentioned
- [ ] Know the address — look it up on Google Maps for any visible exterior issues
- [ ] Charged laptop or tablet with proposal system accessible
- [ ] Material samples for the relevant trade(s)
- [ ] Portfolio loaded (2–3 projects from the same trade the customer needs)
- [ ] DocuSign accessible and ready (for same-day close)

> **Critical**: If only one decision-maker will be present, acknowledge this upfront and plan for a follow-up with both. Never build a full proposal for someone who can't sign.

---

## Phase 1 — Rapport & Discovery (10–15 min)

### Objective
Build trust fast. Understand their situation, priorities, and emotional state before presenting anything.

### The "Walk the Home" Technique
Ask to see the area of concern before sitting down. Walking with them immediately shifts the dynamic — you're a professional assessing their home, not a salesperson pitching at a kitchen table.

**Opening line**:
> "Before we get into anything else — would you mind showing me the area you're most concerned about? I want to make sure I understand what you're dealing with before we go over any options."

### Discovery Questions

Use open questions. Let them talk. Take brief notes.

1. **Pain point**: "What made you decide to look into this now?"
2. **Timeline**: "Is there a particular time frame you're working with?"
3. **Budget signal**: "Have you given any thought to what kind of budget you're working with for this?"
4. **Priority**: "If you had to rank them — price, quality, or getting it done quickly — which matters most to you?"
5. **Past experience**: "Have you worked with contractors before? How did that go?"
6. **Decision process**: "Besides yourself, who else will be involved in making this decision?"

> **Listen for**: urgency signals (roof is leaking, selling the house soon), hesitations (spouse not present, waiting for tax return), and priority alignment (quality-first vs. budget-first).

### What to Confirm Before Moving On

- You understand the primary problem
- You know who the decision-makers are and whether both are present
- You have a sense of whether they're price-sensitive or value-oriented
- You know their timeline

## Phase 2 — Trusted Contractor Presentation (5 min)

### Objective
Establish credibility. This is short and factual — not a sales pitch.

### What to Cover

1. **Who we are**: TPR, how long we've been operating in Southern California, team background
2. **Licensing**: "We're a licensed California contractor — license number [X]. I can show you the certificate."
3. **Insurance**: "We carry general liability and workers' comp on all our crews."
4. **Warranty story**: "We back our work with a [X-year] workmanship warranty, plus the manufacturer's warranty passes through to you."
5. **Portfolio**: Show 2–3 relevant before/after projects. For each, give a one-sentence story: "This customer had the same issue — here's what we did and what they said."

> **Keep this under 5 minutes.** This is not your pitch — it's your credentials. The customer already agreed to meet you. You're just confirming they were right to do so.

---

## Phase 3 — Scope of Work Presentation (15–20 min)

### Objective
Present solutions to their specific problems. Do not present everything TPR does.

### Rules for This Phase

1. **Start with 1–2 scopes only** — their #1 pain point plus one natural complement
2. **Use outcome language, not technical language**: "This will keep your home 15 degrees cooler in summer" not "R-38 blown-in insulation with air sealing"
3. **Tie every scope back to what they told you in discovery**: "You mentioned energy bills were a big concern — this is specifically what addresses that."

### Example Outcome Statements by Trade

| Trade | Outcome statement |
|---|---|
| Roofing | "Your home will be fully protected from weather damage for the next 30 years, and the cool-roof system will reduce attic heat by up to 30%." |
| Insulation | "You'll notice the difference in comfort within the first month, and most customers see their energy bill drop by 20–40%." |
| Windows | "No more drafts, no more noise from outside, and your home will hold temperature much more consistently." |
| HVAC | "Your system will be efficient enough that most customers cut their cooling bill nearly in half." |
| Solar | "You're essentially locking in your energy rate for 25 years — while your neighbors keep seeing their bills go up." |
| Bathroom | "You'll have a completely updated bathroom that feels brand new — and it adds significantly to your home's resale value." |

### Natural Scope Pairings (for upsells)

- Insulation + HVAC (energy savings compound)
- Roofing redeck + Insulation (attic is already accessible)
- Windows + Insulation (envelope sealing — complete comfort story)
- Bathroom + Full home Flooring (natural extension room-by-room)

## Phase 4 — Pricing & Financing (10–15 min)

### Rules for This Phase

1. **Never lead with total price** — always lead with value
2. **Present total price only after scope is agreed** — the customer should want it before you quote it
3. **Immediately bridge to monthly payment** — the moment you say the total
4. **Introduce incentives as a same-day decision driver**

### The Price Bridge Script

> "The total investment for everything we discussed is $[X]. Now, most of our customers don't pay that all at once — we work with financing options that get you into this for around $[monthly payment]/month. That's less than a car payment to have [outcome they care about most]."

Then stop talking. Let that land.

### Incentive Structure

TPR incentives should be:
- **Real** — don't fabricate urgency
- **Time-bound** — expiring at a specific date or tied to a specific condition
- **Meaningful** — a dollar amount, an upgrade, or a free add-on

Example:
> "I do want to mention — we have a promotion running through [end of month]. If we can get the paperwork started today, we can include [incentive] at no additional cost. That's something I can't guarantee next week."

For monthly payment math, see `proposal/financing-presentation.md` and `src/shared/lib/loan-calculations.ts`.

## Phase 5 — Close (10 min)

### The Direct Ask

Do not ask "What do you think?" Ask for the business.

> "Based on everything we've talked about, I think this is a really strong fit for your home. What would you need to feel comfortable moving forward today?"

Or, for the assumptive close:

> "Let's go ahead and get the paperwork started. I can pull up the agreement right now — it only takes a few minutes to go through."

For close technique details, see `sales/closing-strategies.md`.

### If They Have Objections

Address each one specifically. Do not skip them, dismiss them, or just repeat the price.

For scripted responses to each objection, see `sales/objection-handlers.md`.

### If They Are Not Closing Today

Get a specific next step before you leave — not "I'll think about it."

> "I completely understand. When do you think you'll have a chance to sit down together and review this? Let's set a specific time for a quick follow-up call — 15 minutes. I'll send the proposal in the meantime so you have everything in writing."

Do not leave without:
- A proposal email address confirmed
- A specific follow-up date/time agreed

- [ ] Create follow-up task in Monday.com
- [ ] Note any objections, concerns, or things customer emphasized — use these in follow-up
