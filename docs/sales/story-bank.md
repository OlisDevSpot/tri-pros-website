# Story Bank — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Why Stories Matter

Stories are the most powerful tool in sales. Facts tell — stories sell. In the A.R.A.C. objection handling framework, the **Associate** step requires a short, specific story about a past customer who had the same concern and had a positive outcome.

Every rep should have 2-3 stories memorized for each objection category. A story should feel natural, specific, and effortless — like you're telling a friend about something that happened last week.

**Framework reference**: See `sales/sales-frameworks.md` — A.R.A.C. Framework and CLOSER E step.

---

## Story Format

Every story follows the same 4-beat structure. Keep it to 3-4 sentences total.

| Beat | What it covers | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Situation** | Who they were, what they needed — match the current customer's profile | "I worked with a family in Riverside last spring — similar home to yours, similar age roof." |
| **Concern** | The same hesitation the current customer is expressing | "They had the same reaction to the price — it was more than they expected." |
| **Decision** | What they chose to do and why | "They decided to go with financing, and once they saw it was less than their monthly energy bill..." |
| **Outcome** | The specific, measurable result | "...three months later they told me their energy bill dropped by $180 a month. They said it was the best money they ever spent." |

**Rules**:
- Use real customer stories (or composites based on real outcomes)
- Be specific: names (first name or last name with permission), cities, dollar amounts, timeframes
- Match the story to the customer's situation — trade, home type, concern
- Keep it short. A story that runs longer than 30 seconds loses its power.

## Stories by Objection Category

### Category 1: Sticker Shock ("That's more than I expected")

Stories about customers who hesitated on price, committed, and are glad they did.

**Template**:
> "I worked with [name/description] in [city] [time frame]. Same trade, similar home. They had the same reaction to the investment. Once we broke it down to $[monthly]/month, they said it felt much more manageable. [Time] later, they told me [specific positive outcome — bill savings, comfort improvement, property value, peace of mind]."

**Placeholder stories** (replace with real TPR customer data):

1. **Energy savings story**: "The Nguyen family in Rancho Cucamonga — insulation + HVAC, $22,000. They almost walked because of the total. We showed them $189/month financing. Six months later, their energy bill went from $380 to $190. The project is literally paying for itself."

2. **Roof investment story**: "A couple in Corona — full roof replacement, $16,000. They had a quote from another contractor for $9,000. They chose us. Two years later, they had zero issues, full manufacturer warranty intact. Their neighbor who went with the cheaper contractor? Had to redo the entire job within 18 months."

3. **Daily cost reframe**: "A homeowner in Fontana — $14,000 for windows and insulation. When we showed him that was $4.50/day to eliminate his draft problem permanently, he said 'I spend more than that on coffee.'"

### Category 2: Spouse / Partner Objection ("I need to talk to my spouse")

Stories about skeptical partners who came around after hearing the full picture.

**Template**:
> "I totally get that — [name]'s [spouse] said the same thing. We set up a quick call, I walked them both through the proposal, and once [spouse] saw [specific detail that addressed their concern], they were actually the one who said 'let's do it.'"

**Placeholder stories**:

1. "The Martinez family — the wife was all in after the meeting, but her husband wasn't there. We scheduled a 15-minute call. I showed him the warranty and the licensing on our CSLB page. He said, 'That's exactly what I needed to see.' They signed that evening."

2. "A couple in Ontario — the husband came to the meeting alone. His wife was skeptical about spending money on insulation she couldn't see. We sent her a before/after thermal image from a similar project. She called me the next day to ask how soon we could start."

---

### Category 3: Competitor Quote ("I got a cheaper quote")

Stories about customers who went cheap and regretted it — or who chose TPR after comparing.

**Template**:
> "I had a customer in [city] who got a lower quote from another contractor. They went with the cheaper option. [What happened]. They ended up calling us [time later] to redo the work. The 'savings' cost them double."

**Placeholder stories**:

1. "A homeowner in Moreno Valley chose a $7,000 roof job over our $14,000 quote. Eight months later, his garage flooded because the flashing wasn't done right. The other contractor's 'warranty' was worthless — they didn't even have a valid license. He paid us to redo the entire roof. Total cost: $21,000."

2. "A family in San Bernardino got three quotes for HVAC. Ours was in the middle. The cheapest quote didn't include the permit or the load calculation. They chose us. During installation, the city inspector told them their ductwork needed replacement too — something the cheap contractor would have missed because they weren't pulling a permit. We caught it and fixed it."

### Category 4: "I Need to Think About It"

Stories about customers who almost walked, came back, and were glad they did.

**Template**:
> "[Name] said the exact same thing. I sent the proposal, followed up [time later]. When we reconnected, they said [what changed their mind]. They signed and [positive outcome]."

**Placeholder stories**:

1. "A couple in Temecula said they needed to think about it. I sent the proposal and followed up two days later when I saw they'd opened it three times. The wife said, 'We keep coming back to it — we're just nervous about the commitment.' I walked them through the warranty one more time and showed them the financing. They signed that afternoon."

2. "A homeowner in Upland wanted to think about a bathroom remodel. A week later, his shower started leaking into the subfloor. He called me and said, 'I wish I'd said yes when you were here.' We got him scheduled that week."

### Category 5: Energy Savings (Measurable Results)

Stories with specific, quantifiable bill reductions. These are the most powerful for energy-efficient trades.

**Placeholder stories**:

1. "The Patel family in Fontana — insulation + HVAC + windows. Their energy bill went from $420/month to $185/month. $235/month in savings. Their financing payment is $210/month. They're actually cash-positive after 30 days."

2. "A senior couple in Riverside — just insulation and air sealing, $8,500. Their summer bill dropped from $310 to $195. They said the house was more comfortable than it had been in 20 years."

3. "A young family in Eastvale — solar + insulation. They locked in their rate at $0.08/kWh. Their neighbors are paying $0.36/kWh. Over 25 years, that's over $100,000 in savings."

### Category 6: Timeline / Urgency (Waited Too Long)

Stories about customers who delayed and regretted it.

**Placeholder stories**:

1. "A homeowner in Lake Elsinore put off his roof for two years. Last January, a storm took off a section of shingles. Emergency tarp, water damage to the master bedroom ceiling, insurance claim. The emergency repair alone cost more than the full replacement would have."

2. "A family in Murrieta wanted to wait until spring for insulation. That winter, their heating bill hit $500 in December. When they finally called us in March, they said they'd wasted over $1,000 in excess energy costs just by waiting four months."

## How to Build Your Story Bank

### For Agents

1. After every completed project, write down the story in the 4-beat format
2. Categorize it by objection type
3. Practice telling it in under 30 seconds
4. Have at least 2 stories per category memorized at all times

### For the Company

1. Collect stories from all reps quarterly
2. Verify outcomes (actual bill savings, actual timelines)
3. Get customer permission for named stories
4. Update this document with verified, named stories
5. Include the best stories in sales training

### When to Use Stories in the Meeting

Stories are used in two places:
1. **Circle of Pain transition** — after exploring their past experiences, tell a story that mirrors their situation
2. **Objection handling (Associate step)** — when they raise a concern, acknowledge it, reframe it, then associate with a matching story

**Do not use more than 2-3 stories per meeting.** Stories lose power when overused. Pick the ones that most closely match this specific customer's situation.
