# Sales Frameworks — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Overview

This is the canonical reference for the sales frameworks that power TPR's entire customer-facing sales cycle. Every sales doc in this directory builds on the frameworks defined here. When in doubt about methodology, this is the source of truth.

**Core philosophy**: Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust. TPR's sales approach is consultative, not aggressive. The agent educates, diagnoses, and presents — the customer decides.

These frameworks are adapted from Alex Hormozi's body of work ($100M Offers, $100M Leads, CLOSER framework, AAA framework) and integrated with TPR's domain-specific knowledge (due diligence story, SWCE competitive framework, energy-efficient selling, in-home meeting dynamics).

## The CLOSER Framework (Meeting Structure)

The CLOSER framework defines the flow of every in-home meeting. Each letter is a phase — executed in order. Skipping a step or reordering them weakens the close.

**The rule**: Only three things should be said in a meeting — questions, restatements, and stories. Everything else is noise.

### C — Clarify (Why Are You Here?)

Get the customer to own their problem before you present anything.

**Purpose**: Establish why the meeting is happening — in the customer's own words. Until they articulate the problem, you're solving something they haven't admitted to.

**Key questions**:
> "What made you decide to look into this now?"
> "What are we trying to solve here?"
> "What would it mean if this problem just went away?"

**What to listen for**: The emotional driver behind the practical concern. "Our energy bills are crazy" means anxiety about money. "The roof is 25 years old" means fear of catastrophic failure. The emotion is what you'll sell to later.

**Red flag**: "I'm just getting quotes" or "I'm just looking for information." These signal the customer hasn't owned their problem yet. Dig deeper before moving forward.

**Maps to**: Meeting playbook Phase 1 — this is the opening move before any discovery questions.

---

### L — Label (Name Their Situation)

Summarize the customer's situation in clear terms. Make it concrete.

**Purpose**: Transform abstract concerns into a named, diagnosable situation. When the customer hears their problem reflected back accurately, trust accelerates.

**Technique**: After discovery, reflect back what you've heard as a diagnosis — like a doctor explaining what they've found.

**Script**:
> "Here's what I'm seeing: you've got a [X-year-old roof / drafty home / aging HVAC system] that's [costing you $X/month / putting your home at risk / making your family uncomfortable]. You're at the point where doing nothing costs more than fixing it. Does that sound right?"

**Why it matters**: Customers who hear their problem labeled accurately feel understood. Feeling understood builds trust faster than any credential.

**Maps to**: New step between discovery and the trusted contractor presentation. This is where the rep earns the right to present solutions.

---

### O — Overview Past Experiences (The Circle of Pain)

Explore every failed attempt to solve this problem. Dwell here.

**Purpose**: The Circle of Pain builds the case for why TPR is different. Every past failure — bad contractor, DIY gone wrong, deferred maintenance, cheap quote that fell apart — is ammunition for why the customer should do it right this time.

**Key questions**:
> "Have you worked with contractors before? Tell me about that experience."
> "What happened? Why didn't it work out?"
> "What did that end up costing you — in money, time, stress?"
> "What would you have done differently?"

**How to use it**: Don't rush past the answers. Let them tell the full story. Each failure is a pain point that TPR's due diligence story directly addresses. When you later present licensing, supervision, and warranty — they'll connect it to what went wrong before.

**If they have no past experience**: Ask what they've heard from neighbors, friends, or family. "What have you heard about working with contractors?" Most homeowners have at least one horror story secondhand.

**Strategic purpose**: By the time you've explored the Circle of Pain, the customer is emotionally primed for someone who does things differently. This is the perfect setup for the due diligence story.

**Maps to**: New dedicated step in the meeting flow, between discovery and the trusted contractor presentation.

### S — Sell the Vacation (Not the Plane Flight)

Present outcomes, not process. Customers buy destinations, not tickets.

**Purpose**: When you sell a vacation, nobody cares about the boarding process, the airplane model, or the layover schedule. They care about the beach. Home improvement works the same way.

**Core principle**: The more you explain about the technical details of what you're doing, the more ammunition you give the customer to argue or hesitate. Keep it about what their life looks like after.

**Good (vacation)**:
> "You'll notice the comfort difference within the first month. Most customers see their energy bill drop by 20-40%. No more drafts, no more hot spots, no more arguments over the thermostat."

**Bad (plane flight)**:
> "We'll install R-38 blown-in fiberglass with air sealing at all penetration points, and a radiant barrier on the underside of the roof deck."

**Before presenting any scope, recap the C-L-O steps**:
> "So we've talked about what brought you here. You've got [labeled situation]. You've tried [past experiences] and it didn't work. Now you want [desired outcome]. Let me show you how we'd get you there."

**Maps to**: Meeting playbook Phase 4 (Scope Presentation) — every scope item gets outcome language.

**Cross-reference**: `proposal/scope-presentation.md` for outcome statement formulas and examples by trade.

### E — Explain Away Concerns (Handle Objections)

Address every objection. 90% of customers say "no" initially — this is expected.

**The three categories of resistance** (diagnose the type before responding):

| Category | What it sounds like | What it really means | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **External blame** | "The economy," "bad timing," "can't afford it" | Responsibility placed on external factors | Reframe timing/money as the reason TO act, not wait |
| **Other-person dependency** | "Need to talk to my spouse," "my partner decides" | They don't want to own the decision alone | Involve the other person; schedule a call with both |
| **Internal conflict** | "Not sure it's right for me," "what if it fails" | Self-doubt or fear of commitment | Build confidence through guarantees, stories, and clarity |

**Key technique**: Use short anecdotal stories to break pre-existing beliefs. A story about a real customer who had the same concern — and what happened — is more persuasive than any argument. (See `sales/story-bank.md` for the catalog.)

**If they say "I need to think about it"**, confirm three things before accepting:
1. Do they believe the solution addresses their need?
2. Does it align with their values and priorities?
3. Do they have the financial capability?

If any answer is no, that's the real objection. Handle it.

**Maps to**: Meeting playbook Phase 6 (Close) and `sales/objection-handlers.md` for scripted responses.

### R — Reinforce the Decision (Post-Sale)

The sale doesn't end at signature. The first 48 hours are where deals collapse or solidify.

**Purpose**: Buyer's remorse peaks in the first 24-48 hours. Overcommunication during this window reduces cancellations and builds the foundation for referrals.

**The reinforcement sequence**:
1. **Immediately**: Thank you call/text from the agent
2. **Within hours**: Welcome email with a clear "what happens next" guide
3. **Day 1**: Introduction to project manager or production team
4. **Day 2**: Progress update — even if there's nothing new: "Your project is in the queue"
5. **Day 3-5**: Personalized touch (handwritten card, photo of a similar completed project)
6. **Pre-production**: Scheduling confirmation with excitement language

**Principle**: Create 3-7 touchpoints before the next major milestone. Each touchpoint reinforces that they made the right decision.

**Maps to**: `sales/post-signing-sequence.md` for the full protocol and `sales/follow-up-cadence.md` for the pre-signing cadence.

## The A.R.A.C. Framework (Objection Handling)

TPR's evolved objection handling framework — combining the original A.R.C. (Acknowledge, Reframe, Close) with Hormozi's AAA (Acknowledge, Associate, Ask). The result is A.R.A.C. — a four-step process that handles both the logic and the emotion of an objection.

### A — Acknowledge

Validate the concern without agreeing with it. Repeat it back in a neutral tone.

> "I completely understand — that's a significant investment, and I'd feel the same way."

**Why**: Demonstrates listening, builds rapport, buys you thinking time. Never argue, dismiss, or jump to a counter-argument.

### R — Reframe

Shift the frame to your advantage using logic.

> "Most of our customers finance it, which brings it to about $[monthly]/month. That's around $[daily cost]/day to have [outcome]."

**Why**: The logical reframe addresses the rational mind. Price becomes monthly payment. Cost becomes investment. Expense becomes savings.

### A — Associate

Share a story of a past customer who had the same concern and had a positive outcome.

> "Actually, most of our best clients had that same reaction. The Garcia family in Riverside told us the same thing — and three months later they said it was the best money they ever spent because their energy bill dropped by $180 a month."

**Why**: The association addresses the emotional mind. The customer sees themselves in someone else's success story. This is the most persuasive step — facts tell, stories sell.

### C — Close

Move toward the next step. Don't end the objection handling without advancing the conversation.

> "Based on that, does the monthly payment work better for how you're thinking about this?"

**Why**: An objection handled without a close attempt is a wasted opportunity. Every objection is a buying signal — if they weren't interested, they'd just say no.

**Full reference**: `sales/objection-handlers.md` for scripted responses to every common objection.

## The Value Equation (Proposal & Pricing)

How customers unconsciously evaluate whether something is "worth it."

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

### The Four Variables — TPR Context

**INCREASE these (numerator):**

| Variable | What it means | TPR lever |
|---|---|---|
| **Dream Outcome** | The emotional transformation, not the deliverable | Outcome language in proposals: "A home that's comfortable year-round" not "R-38 insulation" |
| **Perceived Likelihood** | How certain the customer is that it will actually work | Due diligence story (6-point checklist), SWCE framework, portfolio, testimonials, warranties, licensing |

**DECREASE these (denominator):**

| Variable | What it means | TPR lever |
|---|---|---|
| **Time Delay** | How fast they see results | Projected start date in proposal, quick-win framing ("comfort difference within the first month"), milestone timeline |
| **Effort & Sacrifice** | How much work the customer has to do | "We handle everything — permits, scheduling, materials, crews, cleanup. You approve the plan and we deliver." DFY positioning |

**The strategic insight**: Most businesses optimize the numerator (dream outcome). The denominator is where the real leverage is. Reducing time delay and effort often has a bigger impact on perceived value than increasing the dream outcome.

**Before sending any proposal, score it**:
- [ ] Does it paint the dream outcome vividly? (not just list scopes)
- [ ] Does it prove it will work? (portfolio, testimonials, guarantees, credentials)
- [ ] Does it show speed? (start date, timeline, milestones)
- [ ] Does it minimize customer effort? (we handle everything)

## The Conviction Framework (Rep Mindset)

**Core principle**: "Selling is a transference of belief over a bridge of trust."

This is not a technique — it's a prerequisite. No script, framework, or process will work if the rep doesn't genuinely believe.

### The Three Beliefs (Before Any Meeting)

The rep must believe — genuinely, not performatively:

1. **The service delivers real results** — TPR's work measurably improves the customer's home, comfort, energy bills, and property value
2. **The customer's life will be better after buying** — this specific customer, for their specific situation, will benefit
3. **NOT buying would be a disservice** — the customer will end up with an unlicensed contractor, deferred maintenance, or continued waste

### Why Conviction Matters Twice

Conviction drives two things:
- **Persuasion**: Customers can sense whether you believe what you're telling them. Tonality is 90% of selling — words are 10%.
- **Work ethic**: A rep who believes follows up faster, pushes through objections harder, and doesn't give up after one "no."

### Practical Application

Before every meeting, the rep should ask themselves:
> "If this customer doesn't hire us, what happens to them? Who do they end up hiring? What goes wrong?"

If the answer is "they'll probably hire an unlicensed contractor and get burned" — then closing is not pressure, it's protection.

## Grand Slam Offer Principles (Proposal Design)

Hormozi's Grand Slam Offer framework is about making "offers so good people feel stupid saying no." Applied to TPR proposals:

### Offer Stacking

Instead of presenting a single line-item scope, break it into named components and stack them:

| Instead of | Stack it as |
|---|---|
| "Roof replacement — $18,000" | "The Complete Protection Package: 30-year architectural shingle system + new underlayment + updated flashing + ridge vent upgrade + full permit and inspection + 10-year workmanship warranty + manufacturer warranty pass-through" |

Each component named separately increases perceived value — even though the deliverable is identical.

### Offer Enhancers

| Enhancer | TPR Application |
|---|---|
| **Scarcity** (quantity) | "We only take on X projects per month in your area" / "Our roofing crew has one opening in [month]" |
| **Urgency** (time) | Real incentive expiration, seasonal timing, booking backlog, material availability |
| **Bonuses** | Reframe existing inclusions: "Free energy audit (valued at $X)", "Complimentary post-installation inspection", "Included: full permit and city inspection" |
| **Guarantees** | Name the guarantee: "The Tri Pros Peace of Mind Guarantee" — workmanship warranty + dedicated PM + post-completion walkthrough |
| **Naming** | Use the M.A.G.I.C. formula: Magnetic reason + Announce avatar + Give goal + Indicate time + Container word. Example: "The Energy-Saver+ Program" |

### The 1-2-3 Scope Rule as Offer Architecture

TPR's existing 1-2-3 Scope Rule is a natural Grand Slam Offer structure:
1. **Core offer** = primary scope (the main thing they need)
2. **Bonus stack** = complementary scope (positioned as an added value, not an upsell)
3. **Optional add-ons** = everything else (low-pressure, customer-initiated)

## The Circle of Pain (Discovery Technique)

A dedicated technique within the CLOSER O step. Not just "have you worked with contractors before?" — it's a deep dive into every failed attempt.

### Why It Works

Every failed past experience is:
- **Emotional fuel** — the customer re-experiences frustration, anxiety, and regret
- **Setup for your pitch** — "That's exactly why we do X differently"
- **Self-persuasion** — the customer convinces themselves they need someone better this time

### How to Execute

1. **Ask the opening question**: "Have you ever had work done on your home before? How did that go?"
2. **Go deeper on failures**: "What went wrong? What did that end up costing you?"
3. **Explore all attempts**: "Was there anything else you tried before calling us?"
4. **Exhaust the list**: Keep asking until they have nothing left. "Anything else?"
5. **Summarize the pattern**: "So you've tried X and Y, and both times Z happened. That's exactly what I hear from a lot of homeowners."

### The Transition to Due Diligence

After the Circle of Pain, the due diligence story enters naturally:

> "That's why we built our process around six things that prevent exactly what you described. Can I walk you through them?"

The customer is now emotionally primed to hear about licensing, scope clarity, supervision, communication, office support, and proof of performance — because they just relived what happens without them.

**Maps to**: `sales/due-diligence-story.md` and meeting playbook Phase 2.
