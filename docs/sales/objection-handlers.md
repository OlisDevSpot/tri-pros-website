# Objection Handlers — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Framework: A.R.C.

Every objection response follows the same structure:

- **A — Acknowledge**: Validate their concern without agreeing with it
- **R — Reframe**: Shift the frame to your advantage
- **C — Close**: Move toward a next step

Never argue. Never dismiss. Never repeat the price louder.

---

## Objection 1: "That's more than I expected" (Sticker Shock)

**When it happens**: Right after you present the total price.

**What it usually means**: They were mentally anchored to a lower number, or they haven't yet connected the price to the value.

### Response Script

> "I completely understand — it's a significant investment, and I'd feel the same way. Can I put it in perspective for a second?
>
> The total is $[X]. Most of our customers finance it, which brings it to about $[monthly]/month. Over [loan term] years, that's around $[daily cost]/day to have [primary outcome they care about].
>
> Compare that to what you're spending right now on [problem being solved — higher energy bills, leak repairs, etc.] — and the difference is actually pretty small. Does the monthly work better for how you're thinking about this?"

### Backup Move

If they're still stuck on total price:

> "What if I could adjust the scope slightly to bring the total down? What's the number you'd be comfortable with, and we can work backwards from there."

Then identify what scope could be removed or phased. Never discount arbitrarily — restructure the scope.

### Introduce the Incentive

> "Also — I should mention, I have an incentive I can apply if we can get the paperwork started today. That brings the number down to $[adjusted total] / $[adjusted monthly]. Does that help?"

## Objection 2: "I need to talk to my spouse / partner"

**When it happens**: At the close, when only one decision-maker is present.

**What it usually means**: Either the other person is genuinely needed, or this is a polite stall.

### Prevention (Best Strategy)

Before the meeting, confirm both decision-makers will be present:

> "Just want to make sure — will your husband/wife/partner be home during our visit? I ask because we'll be going through everything in detail, and it's really helpful to have both of you there so no one has to play telephone with the information."

### If It Still Happens — Response Script

> "Of course — absolutely the right call. I wouldn't want you making a decision this size without your partner's input.
>
> Here's what I'd suggest: I'll send the proposal over right now so you both have everything in writing. Would it make sense to schedule a quick 15-minute call — with both of you — so I can answer any questions your partner might have? That way you're not trying to explain all the details yourself.
>
> What does [spouse/partner]'s schedule look like this week?"

### What You Must Leave With

- A specific date and time for the follow-up call with both people
- Proposal sent to both email addresses if possible
- Not "I'll let you know" — that is not a next step

> "I want to be respectful of your time. Let's put something on the calendar right now. Does tomorrow evening work, or is the weekend better?"

## Objection 3: "I got a cheaper quote from another contractor"

**When it happens**: Before, during, or after the meeting — often at the close.

**What it usually means**: They want a lower price or need reassurance that the difference is worth it.

### Response Script

> "That makes complete sense — you're doing your homework, which is exactly what you should do.
>
> Can I ask a couple of quick questions about that other quote? Do you know if they're licensed with the CSLB? Are they carrying general liability and workers' comp on their crews? And what kind of warranty do they offer on the workmanship?
>
> I ask because a lower price often reflects what's not included. No license means no legal protection for you if something's not up to code. No workers' comp means you're personally exposed if someone gets hurt on your property. And no manufacturer-certified installation means your material warranty is voided — even if they use the same brand product we'd use.
>
> When you account for those protections, our number is actually very competitive for what you're getting."

### The "Apples to Apples" Closer

> "I'd never ask you to pay more for the same thing. But this isn't the same thing. Would you feel comfortable if I walked you through exactly what's included in our quote that likely isn't in theirs?"

Then walk through the S-W-C-E framework from `company/competitive-advantage.md`.

### Last Resort

If they insist the other contractor is legitimate and priced lower:

> "I respect that, and ultimately you should work with who you're most comfortable with. What I can tell you is that if anything goes wrong — and in this industry, callbacks happen — you will want someone with a license, insurance, and a warranty behind them. That's the only way this story ends well for you.
>
> If you'd like, I can come down slightly on the scope to match your budget — but I won't be able to match a price that doesn't include what we include."

## Objection 4: "I'm confused / overwhelmed about what's included"

**When it happens**: After the scope presentation, or when reviewing the proposal.

**What it usually means**: You presented too much at once, or the language was too technical.

### Response Script

> "That's completely fair — let me simplify this.
>
> The two most important things for your home right now are [primary scope] and [secondary scope]. Everything else on the proposal is additional but optional. Let's focus on just those two and make sure they make sense before we go any further."

Then re-explain those two scopes using outcome language only. No technical specs. No product names. Just what it means for their home and their life.

### For Proposal Review (Remote Confusion)

> "I'd love to walk through this with you on a quick screen share. It takes 15 minutes and I'll explain each section so you know exactly what you're getting. Does that work?"

A live walkthrough almost always resolves scope confusion and re-warms a cold proposal.

## Objection 5: "I need to think about it"

**When it happens**: At the close — often used as a polite refusal or genuine delay.

**What it usually means**: There is an unresolved concern they haven't named yet.

### Step 1 — Uncover the Real Objection

> "Of course — this is a big decision. Can I ask what specifically you'd want to think through? Is it the price, the scope, the timing — or something else? I want to make sure I've addressed everything before you make a decision."

Let them answer. The real objection usually surfaces here.

Then handle that objection with the appropriate script above.

### Step 2 — If There's No Specific Concern

> "I understand. What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable moving forward?"

This forces them to articulate the condition. If they can't name one, you can gently push:

> "Sometimes 'I need to think' means 'I'm not sure it's the right fit' — and that's okay to say. Is there something about this that doesn't feel right?"

### Step 3 — Never Leave Without a Next Step

> "I respect that. Let's do this: I'll send the proposal right now so you have everything in writing. When do you think you'll have a chance to review it — tomorrow? Thursday? Let's set a time to connect briefly and I can answer any questions."

**Required before you leave**:
- Specific follow-up date and time agreed
- Proposal sent / confirmed they have it
- Note what they said they needed to think about

For follow-up cadence after "I need to think about it," see `sales/follow-up-cadence.md`.

## General Principles

1. **Every objection is a buying signal** — if they weren't interested, they'd just say no
2. **Ask before you answer** — clarify what they actually mean before responding
3. **Never discount without restructuring scope** — arbitrary discounts destroy value and signal that you were overcharging
4. **Always end with a next step** — a vague "I'll follow up" is not a next step
5. **Both decision-makers must be in the room (or on the call) before any close attempt**
