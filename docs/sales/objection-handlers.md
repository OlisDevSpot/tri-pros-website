# Objection Handlers — Tri Pros Remodeling

## Framework: A.R.A.C.

Every objection response follows the same four-step structure:

- **A — Acknowledge**: Validate their concern without agreeing with it
- **R — Reframe**: Shift the frame to your advantage using logic
- **A — Associate**: Tell a short story of a customer who had the same concern and had a positive outcome
- **C — Close**: Move toward a next step

Never argue. Never dismiss. Never repeat the price louder.

A.R.A.C. evolves TPR's original A.R.C. framework by adding the **Associate** step — a story-based technique that addresses the emotional side of objections. Facts tell, stories sell.

**Full framework reference**: See `sales/sales-frameworks.md` — A.R.A.C. Framework.

## Step Zero: Diagnose the Objection Category

Before choosing a response, identify the TYPE of objection. Every sales objection falls into one of three categories, and each requires a different approach.

| Category | What it sounds like | What it really means | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| **External blame** | "Can't afford it," "The economy," "Bad timing," "Too expensive" | Responsibility placed on external factors; they don't yet see the value | Reframe timing/money as the reason TO act, not wait |
| **Other-person dependency** | "Need to talk to my spouse," "My partner decides," "Let me check with..." | They don't want to own the decision alone; or the other person genuinely needs to be involved | Involve the other person; schedule a call with both |
| **Internal conflict** | "I'm not sure," "What if it doesn't work," "I need to think about it" | Self-doubt, fear of commitment, or an unresolved concern they haven't named | Build confidence through guarantees, stories, and process clarity |

**Why this matters**: Treating a "can't afford it" (external) the same as an "I'm not sure" (internal) wastes everyone's time. The reframe that works for price won't work for self-doubt.

## Objection 1: "That's more than I expected" (Sticker Shock)

**Category**: External blame

**When it happens**: Right after you present the total price.

**What it usually means**: They were mentally anchored to a lower number, or they haven't yet connected the price to the value.

### A.R.A.C. Response

**Acknowledge**:
> "I completely understand — it's a significant investment, and I'd feel the same way."

**Reframe** (bridge to monthly payment):
> "Can I put it in perspective for a second? The total is $[X]. Most of our customers finance it, which brings it to about $[monthly]/month. Over [loan term] years, that's around $[daily cost]/day to have [primary outcome they care about].
>
> Compare that to what you're spending right now on [problem being solved — higher energy bills, leak repairs, etc.] — and the difference is actually pretty small."

**Associate** (story from `sales/story-bank.md`):
> "Actually, the Garcia family in Riverside had the exact same reaction. Once they saw the monthly payment and compared it to their energy bill, they said it was a no-brainer. Three months later, their bill dropped by $180 a month."

**Close**:
> "Does the monthly work better for how you're thinking about this?"

### Backup Move

If they're still stuck on total price:

> "What if I could adjust the scope slightly to bring the total down? What's the number you'd be comfortable with, and we can work backwards from there."

Then identify what scope could be removed or phased. Never discount arbitrarily — restructure the scope.

### Introduce the Incentive

> "Also — I should mention, I have an incentive I can apply if we can get the paperwork started today. That brings the number down to $[adjusted total] / $[adjusted monthly]. Does that help?"

## Objection 2: "I need to talk to my spouse / partner"

**Category**: Other-person dependency

**When it happens**: At the close, when only one decision-maker is present.

**What it usually means**: Either the other person is genuinely needed, or this is a polite stall.

### Prevention (Best Strategy)

Before the meeting, confirm both decision-makers will be present:

> "Just want to make sure — will your husband/wife/partner be home during our visit? I ask because we'll be going through everything in detail, and it's really helpful to have both of you there so no one has to play telephone with the information."

### A.R.A.C. Response

**Acknowledge**:
> "Of course — absolutely the right call. I wouldn't want you making a decision this size without your partner's input."

**Reframe**:
> "Here's what I'd suggest: I'll send the proposal over right now so you both have everything in writing. Would it make sense to schedule a quick 15-minute call — with both of you — so I can answer any questions your partner might have? That way you're not trying to explain all the details yourself."

**Associate**:
> "The Martinez family had the same thing happen — the husband wasn't at the meeting. We set up a call, I showed him the license and warranty, and he said 'That's exactly what I needed to see.' They signed that evening."

**Close**:
> "What does [spouse/partner]'s schedule look like this week? Let's put something on the calendar right now."

### What You Must Leave With

- A specific date and time for the follow-up call with both people
- Proposal sent to both email addresses if possible
- Not "I'll let you know" — that is not a next step

## Objection 3: "I got a cheaper quote from another contractor"

**Category**: External blame

**When it happens**: Before, during, or after the meeting — often at the close.

**What it usually means**: They want a lower price or need reassurance that the difference is worth it.

### A.R.A.C. Response

**Acknowledge**:
> "That makes complete sense — you're doing your homework, which is exactly what you should do."

**Reframe** (the "Apples to Apples" approach):
> "Can I ask a couple of quick questions about that other quote? Do you know if they're licensed with the CSLB? Are they carrying general liability and workers' comp on their crews? And what kind of warranty do they offer on the workmanship?
>
> I ask because a lower price often reflects what's not included. No license means no legal protection for you if something's not up to code. No workers' comp means you're personally exposed if someone gets hurt on your property. And no manufacturer-certified installation means your material warranty is voided — even if they use the same brand product we'd use.
>
> When you account for those protections, our number is actually very competitive for what you're getting."

**Associate**:
> "A homeowner in Moreno Valley chose a $7,000 roof job over our $14,000 quote. Eight months later, the flashing failed and his garage flooded. No warranty, no recourse. He ended up paying us to redo the whole thing — total cost $21,000."

**Close**:
> "I'd never ask you to pay more for the same thing. But this isn't the same thing. Would you feel comfortable if I walked you through exactly what's included in our quote that likely isn't in theirs?"

Then walk through the S-W-C-E framework from `company/competitive-advantage.md`.

### Last Resort

If they insist the other contractor is legitimate and priced lower:

> "I respect that, and ultimately you should work with who you're most comfortable with. What I can tell you is that if anything goes wrong — and in this industry, callbacks happen — you will want someone with a license, insurance, and a warranty behind them. That's the only way this story ends well for you.
>
> If you'd like, I can come down slightly on the scope to match your budget — but I won't be able to match a price that doesn't include what we include."

## Objection 4: "I'm confused / overwhelmed about what's included"

**Category**: Internal conflict

**When it happens**: After the scope presentation, or when reviewing the proposal.

**What it usually means**: You presented too much at once, or the language was too technical.

### A.R.A.C. Response

**Acknowledge**:
> "That's completely fair — and that's on me, not you."

**Reframe** (simplify to 1-2 scopes):
> "Let me simplify this. The two most important things for your home right now are [primary scope] and [secondary scope]. Everything else on the proposal is additional but optional. Let's focus on just those two and make sure they make sense before we go any further."

Then re-explain those two scopes using outcome language only. No technical specs. No product names. Just what it means for their home and their life.

**Associate**:
> "A lot of our customers feel the same way at first — there's a lot of information. The Patel family said the same thing, but once we simplified it to the two things that mattered most, they said it all clicked."

**Close**:
> "Does that make more sense? Should we focus on just those two?"

### For Proposal Review (Remote Confusion)

> "I'd love to walk through this with you on a quick screen share. It takes 15 minutes and I'll explain each section so you know exactly what you're getting. Does that work?"

A live walkthrough almost always resolves scope confusion and re-warms a cold proposal.

## Objection 5: "I need to think about it"

**Category**: Internal conflict (usually — but diagnose first)

**When it happens**: At the close — often used as a polite refusal or genuine delay.

**What it usually means**: There is an unresolved concern they haven't named yet.

### Step 1 — Confirm Three Things (CLOSER E technique)

Before accepting "I need to think about it," check whether they actually have unresolved concerns:

> "Of course — and I respect that. Before you go, can I just confirm three quick things?
> 1. Do you feel like what we presented addresses the issue you're dealing with?
> 2. Does this feel aligned with what's important to you — quality, protection, comfort?
> 3. Is the financial side manageable with the monthly option we discussed?"

If they say **no** to any of these, that's the real objection. Handle it with the appropriate script above.

If they say **yes to all three** but still want to think — the concern is usually fear of commitment (internal conflict).

### Step 2 — Uncover the Real Objection

> "I totally get it. Can I ask — what specifically would you want to think through? Is it the price, the scope, the timing — or something else?"

Let them answer. The real objection usually surfaces here.

### Step 3 — A.R.A.C. on the Real Objection

**Acknowledge**: Whatever they said.

**Reframe**: Address the specific concern.

**Associate**: Story of a customer who felt the same way.
> "A couple in Temecula said the exact same thing. They took a few days, kept coming back to the proposal, and eventually said they were just nervous about the commitment. Once we walked through the warranty one more time, they signed and haven't looked back."

**Close**:
> "What would need to be true for you to feel comfortable moving forward?"

### Step 4 — Never Leave Without a Next Step

> "I respect that. Let's do this: I'll send the proposal right now so you have everything in writing. When do you think you'll have a chance to review it — tomorrow? Thursday? Let's set a time to connect briefly so I can answer any questions."

**Required before you leave**:
- Specific follow-up date and time agreed
- Proposal sent / confirmed they have it
- Note what they said they needed to think about

For follow-up cadence after "I need to think about it," see `sales/follow-up-cadence.md`.

## General Principles

1. **Every objection is a buying signal** — if they weren't interested, they'd just say no
2. **Diagnose the category first** — external, other-person, or internal determines your approach
3. **Ask before you answer** — clarify what they actually mean before responding
4. **Use stories, not arguments** — the Associate step is often more persuasive than the Reframe
5. **Never discount without restructuring scope** — arbitrary discounts destroy value and signal that you were overcharging
6. **Always end with a next step** — a vague "I'll follow up" is not a next step
7. **Both decision-makers must be in the room (or on the call) before any close attempt**
